Living with Functional Nurological Disorder-It's Okay to Not Be Okay



This webpage provides information for people with Functional Neurological Disorder and its symptoms, their carers and their health professionals.


About Functional Nurological Disorder

  • To help you understand the condition, we are going to break down its name:

    FUNCTION: is how your body works>
    NEUROLOGICAL: is anything to do with your nerves and the messages they send to our brains
    DISORDER: is when something doesn›t work as well as it should.

  • Functional Neurological Disorder (FND) is a condition where the brain struggles to send and receive signals correctly, leading to real and often distressing symptoms. These symptoms may include changes in movement, sensation, speech, or cognition, and they occur without structural damage to the nervous system.

  • FND is not imagined or “all in the head”—it is a genuine disorder that affects how the nervous system functions. People with FND may experience sudden weakness, tremors, non-epileptic seizures, or difficulty speaking, among other symptoms. These experiences are valid and deserve understanding, not judgment.

  • The good news is that FND is treatable. With the right support—such as therapy, education, and stress management—many people see improvement. Recovery is possible, and every step forward matters.

  • This site is here to help you understand FND, recognize its signs, and explore ways to manage it with compassion and clarity.

Symptoms

  • The symptoms that you may be experiencing are called Functional Neurological Symptoms (FNS). They are real and genuine and can be debilitating to those experiencing them.

    • The most common symptoms are:

    - Motor Dysfunction

    • Sudden weakness or paralysis
    • Non-epileptic seizures
    • Speech or vision changes
    • Unexplained tremors or movement issues

    - Sensory Dysfunction

    • Functional sensory disturbance includes altered sensation e.g. numbness, tingling or pain in the face, torso or limbs. This often occurs on one side of the body

    • Functional visual symptoms including loss of vision or double vision.

    Other physical and psychological symptoms are commonly experienced by patients with FND but may not be
    present. These include:

    • Chronic pains
    • Bowel and bladder symptoms
    • Fatigue
    • Anxiety
    • Memory symptoms
    • Sleep problems
    • Depression

    Managing Stress

  • Mindfulness

    • Practice breathing exercises and meditation to calm the nervous system.

  • Therapy

    • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) can help reframe negative thoughts and reduce symptoms.

  • Support

    • Connect with FND communities and professionals who understand your journey.
